# Caring for cuts, scrapes, and wounds

Most small wounds heal well at home with simple, clean care.

## Cleaning and dressing a minor wound
1. Wash your hands with soap before touching the wound.
2. Stop light bleeding by pressing with a clean cloth for a few minutes.
3. Rinse the wound under clean running water; wash around it with mild soap. Avoid pouring alcohol or iodine directly into a deep wound — it damages tissue.
4. Remove visible dirt or small debris with cleaned tweezers.
5. Pat dry and cover with a clean bandage or cloth. Change the dressing daily or when it gets wet or dirty.

## Signs a wound is infected
- Increasing pain, swelling, or warmth after the first day or two
- Redness spreading out from the wound
- Pus (thick yellow or green fluid) or a bad smell
- Fever

An infected wound needs medical attention — do not wait for it to worsen.

## Tetanus
Deep wounds, animal bites, and wounds contaminated with soil or manure carry tetanus risk. If the injured person has not had a tetanus vaccination in the last 10 years (5 for dirty wounds), they should get one from a clinic.

## When to seek care immediately
- Bleeding that does not stop with 10 minutes of firm pressure
- A wound that is deep, gaping, or exposes fat or muscle (may need stitches within 24 hours)
- Animal or human bites — these infect easily
- A wound on the face, joint, hand, or genitals
- Numbness or inability to move the area
