# Malaria and dengue (mosquito-borne fevers)

In tropical regions, a fever may be caused by mosquito-borne diseases such as malaria or dengue. Both need medical attention — do not treat a persistent tropical fever as a simple flu.

## Malaria
- Spread by Anopheles mosquitoes, which mostly bite between dusk and dawn
- Symptoms: cycles of fever, chills and heavy sweating, headache, body aches, sometimes vomiting; usually 7-30 days after the bite
- Malaria is diagnosed with a blood test and treated with antimalarial medicines — get tested at a clinic as soon as possible
- Untreated malaria can become life-threatening quickly, especially in children and pregnant women

## Dengue
- Spread by Aedes mosquitoes, which bite mostly during the day
- Symptoms: sudden high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, strong muscle and joint pain, rash, nausea
- There is no specific cure; treatment is rest, plenty of fluids, and paracetamol for fever
- Do NOT take ibuprofen or aspirin with suspected dengue — they increase bleeding risk

## Dengue warning signs (go to a clinic immediately)
- Severe belly pain or persistent vomiting
- Bleeding gums or nose, blood in vomit or stool
- Extreme tiredness or restlessness
- Cold, clammy skin — especially 1-2 days after the fever drops

## Prevention
- Sleep under insecticide-treated bed nets
- Wear long sleeves and trousers at biting times
- Remove standing water around the home (buckets, tyres, pots) where mosquitoes breed
- Use repellent when available
