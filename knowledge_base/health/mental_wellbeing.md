# Stress, anxiety, and low mood

Mental health is part of health. Stress, worry, and sadness are normal responses to hard situations — but when they persist for weeks and interfere with daily life, they deserve care, just like a physical illness.

## Common signs that stress or low mood is becoming serious
- Feeling sad, empty, or hopeless most of the day, most days, for more than 2 weeks
- Losing interest in things you used to enjoy
- Sleeping much more or much less than usual; constant tiredness
- Trouble concentrating or making decisions
- Feeling worthless or excessively guilty
- Constant worry, restlessness, racing heart, or trouble breathing when anxious

## Things that genuinely help
- Keep a daily routine: regular sleep and wake times, regular meals
- Physical activity — even a 20-30 minute walk most days measurably improves mood
- Stay connected: talk to someone you trust; isolation makes everything worse
- Limit alcohol; it deepens depression and disturbs sleep
- Slow breathing for anxiety: breathe in for 4 counts, out for 6, for a few minutes
- Break problems into one small step you can take today

## When to seek help
Talk to a health worker if low mood or anxiety lasts more than two weeks, or sooner if it is severe. Depression and anxiety are treatable.

If someone talks about ending their life, has a plan, or has harmed themselves: do not leave them alone, remove dangerous items, and get help from a health worker or trusted community leader immediately. Asking someone directly about suicidal thoughts does not "put the idea in their head" — it opens the door to help.
