# Muscle cramps and leg cramps

A muscle cramp is a sudden, involuntary tightening of a muscle. Leg cramps — in the calf, foot, or thigh — are very common, and often strike at night ("nocturnal leg cramps"), waking you with a sharp, gripping pain. Most cramps are harmless and pass within seconds to a few minutes, though the muscle can feel tender afterwards.

## Common causes
- Dehydration, or loss of salts through heavy sweating
- Overuse, hard exercise, or holding a position too long
- Sitting or lying awkwardly, or pointing the toes during sleep
- Not enough minerals such as potassium, magnesium, or calcium in the diet
- Pregnancy
- Some medicines (for example certain water pills / diuretics)
- More common in older adults

## What to do during a cramp
1. Gently stretch the muscle. For a calf cramp, straighten the leg and pull the toes up toward the shin.
2. Massage the muscle with your hands.
3. Stand up and walk around, or press the foot flat on the floor.
4. Apply warmth (a warm cloth) to a tight muscle, or cold to a sore one afterwards.

## Preventing cramps
- Drink enough water through the day, especially in heat or after exercise
- Stretch the calves and legs before bed and after activity
- Eat a varied diet with fruit, vegetables, beans, and nuts (for potassium and magnesium)
- Keep bedding loose so the feet aren't forced to point down at night

## When to see a health worker
Most cramps need no treatment. Seek care if cramps:
- Happen often and disrupt sleep or daily life
- Are very painful, or the muscle stays swollen, red, or weak afterwards
- Come with numbness, or the leg is swollen, warm, and tender (a swollen, painful calf can signal a blood clot — get medical help promptly)
- Follow starting a new medicine
- Occur with vomiting, diarrhea, or heavy sweating that could cause dehydration
